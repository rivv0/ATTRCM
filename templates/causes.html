Causes of ATTR-CM

Understanding the origins of Amyloid Transthyretin Cardiomyopathy

The Role of Transthyretin (TTR)

Transthyretin (TTR) is a protein primarily produced by the liver. Its main function is to transport thyroid hormone and vitamin A (retinol) throughout the body. In its normal state, TTR exists as a tetramerâ€”four identical protein subunits bound together.

Normal tetrameric structure of transthyretin protein

Protein Misfolding: The Root Cause

ATTR-CM occurs when the TTR protein becomes unstable and dissociates from its tetrameric structure into monomers. These monomers then misfold and aggregate, forming amyloid fibrils that deposit in the heart tissue.

This process leads to:

  • Thickening of the heart walls (cardiac hypertrophy)
  • Stiffening of the heart muscle (reduced compliance)
  • Disruption of electrical conduction
  • Impaired cardiac function

Hereditary ATTR-CM

Hereditary ATTR-CM is caused by mutations in the TTR gene, which is located on chromosome 18. Over 120 different mutations have been identified, with varying effects on disease onset, progression, and organ involvement.

Key aspects of hereditary ATTR-CM include:

  • Autosomal dominant inheritance pattern (only one copy of the mutated gene is needed to cause the disease)
  • Variable penetrance (not everyone with the mutation will develop the disease)
  • Different mutations are associated with different ethnic groups and geographic regions
  • The most common mutation in the United States is V122I, which affects approximately 3-4% of African Americans

Wild-type ATTR-CM

Wild-type ATTR-CM, previously known as senile systemic amyloidosis, is not caused by a genetic mutation. Instead, it is associated with aging and the natural tendency of the TTR protein to become unstable over time.

Characteristics of wild-type ATTR-CM include:

  • Predominantly affects men over the age of 65
  • Increasing prevalence with age
  • No genetic predisposition
  • Often associated with carpal tunnel syndrome, spinal stenosis, and biceps tendon rupture

Risk Factors

Several factors may increase the risk of developing ATTR-CM:

  • Age: The risk of wild-type ATTR-CM increases significantly after age 65
  • Gender: Wild-type ATTR-CM is more common in men
  • Ethnicity: Certain mutations are more common in specific ethnic groups
  • Family history: Having a family member with hereditary ATTR-CM increases risk
