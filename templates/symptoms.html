Symptoms of ATTR-CM

Recognizing the signs of Amyloid Transthyretin Cardiomyopathy

Cardiac Symptoms

The primary symptoms of ATTR-CM are related to heart failure and include:

Shortness of Breath

Initially with exertion, but eventually even at rest (dyspnea)

Fatigue

Persistent tiredness and reduced exercise capacity

Edema

Swelling in the legs, ankles, and feet due to fluid buildup

Palpitations

Awareness of abnormal heart rhythms or racing heartbeat

Dizziness/Fainting

Due to reduced blood flow to the brain (syncope)

Orthopnea

Difficulty breathing when lying flat

Extra-Cardiac Symptoms

ATTR-CM can also affect other organ systems, leading to a variety of symptoms:

Neurological

  • Peripheral neuropathy (numbness, tingling, pain in extremities)
  • Autonomic dysfunction (dizziness, digestive issues)
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome

Gastrointestinal

  • Nausea and early satiety
  • Diarrhea or constipation
  • Unintentional weight loss

Musculoskeletal

  • Spinal stenosis
  • Biceps tendon rupture
  • Joint pain

Other

  • Bruising easily
  • Macroglossia (enlarged tongue)
  • Bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome

Red Flags for ATTR-CM

The following combination of symptoms and conditions should raise suspicion for ATTR-CM:

  • Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction
  • Bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome (especially if it preceded heart failure symptoms by several years)
  • Spinal stenosis
  • Intolerance to standard heart failure medications
  • Unexplained left ventricular hypertrophy on imaging
  • Low voltage on ECG despite increased wall thickness on imaging

Symptom Progression

ATTR-CM is a progressive disease, with symptoms typically worsening over time:

Early

Mild fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance

Subtle shortness of breath with exertion

Possible early signs of neuropathy

Intermediate

More pronounced heart failure symptoms

Edema requiring diuretics

Arrhythmias may develop

Progressive neuropathy in hereditary forms

Advanced

Severe heart failure symptoms even at rest

Cachexia (muscle wasting)

Significant functional limitation

Potentially life-threatening arrhythmias

When to Seek Medical Attention

You should consult a healthcare provider if you experience:

  • Persistent shortness of breath, especially if it's worsening
  • Swelling in your legs, ankles, or feet that doesn't improve with rest
  • Fatigue that significantly affects your daily activities
  • Palpitations or irregular heartbeat
  • Fainting episodes
  • A combination of heart failure symptoms and a history of carpal tunnel syndrome
